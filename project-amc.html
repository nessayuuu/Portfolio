Design Project -

Project Overview
The Appalachian Mountain Club (AMC) is a non-governmental organization that is focused on preserving the Appalachian Mountain trail for the public. On top of preservation, AMC offers a wide range activities to participate in: from volunteer opportunties to paid excursions - suitable for children to adults.
View Project

Project Type

Collaborative team effort

Role

Designer and researcher.

Duration

Two Weeks

Scope

Shift focus to donations and volunteering.

Problem Statement.

Information presented by the site is disorganized, overwhelming and inconsistent.
The goal is to prioritize what is on the site and reorganize it according to those priorities then
apply it to the redesign process. Doing so allows information to be presented in a clear and concise manner.

User Personas.

To gain a better understanding of the individuals who would participate at AMC,
our team created two user personas: Patrick Hendrickson and Patricia Swanson.

Patrick Hendrickson -

is a single 35 year-old business manager. His hobbies include
shiking, camping, climbing and nature photography. To complement Patrick’s hobbies, his needs include: living a sustainable lifestyle, utilizing eco-friendly camping and climbing gear, and discovering new outdoor activities that will challenge his skills. In addition, Patrick does face some frustrations such as poor environmental and animal conservation efforts from organizations, and lack of transparency from those conservation organizations. Despite all this, he wants to continue the effort in volunteering and giving back through other means when time isn’t available to him for an organization that is transparent.

Patricia Swanson -

is a married 33 year-old financial analyst. Her hobbies include hiking, camping,
going to the beach, taking her family and dogs out for walks and hikes. Patricia finds
it helpful when she is informed, and there is financial transparency so she can
research and plan ahead. When information is inaccessible or outdated, she finds it
extremely frustrating as that can mean being misinformed, and last-minute changes.
To remedy this, Patricia believes modern solutions to outdated problems is the answer.
Including, information that is straight to the point and convenient opportunities
to get involved with incentives for her family.

The Process.

Define.

Our first step was to define the goals of AMC’s website. To define the goals, we created a survey, conducted both a stakeholder interview with an employee at AMC and user interviews with the general public. From there we were able to define three goals: donate, volunteer, and be an all-encompassing resource for the Appalachian Mountain Trails.

Prototype.

The next step was to develop website and mobile wireframes for user testing. Our team condensed the navigation bar options to five items: donate, volunteer, join, resources, and contact. Each item would have a dropdown where it would direct either Patrick or Patricia to their desired goal. The website wireframe focused on volunteering while the mobile focused on providing trail resources.

User Test Outcomes.

We took our initial wireframes and asked random individuals to test it and give us feedback. For the website wireframe, there were two major flaws: naming items and vague call to actions. One example of a naming issue is “Volunteer Resources”, we renamed it “Volunteer Opportunities” as many testers found the term resources to be either too vague or mean something else completely. Our testers also encountered issues where they wanted to volunteer, but could not find the button to do so with ease. So our team went back and moved the volunteer button around so it was more easily accessible.

Despite the issues we encountered, most users enjoyed the location feature of volunteering. As the Appalachian Mountain Trail spans 14 states, it can be difficult to narrow down volunteering opportunities for an individual residing in one state.

For our mobile wireframes, our two major flaws was once again naming items, but also, the order of the dropdown menu. During testing, users couldn’t find where the trail resources were but guessed from process of elimination that “Resources” meant “Trails” at the bottom of the menu. From there, our team eliminated “Resources” from the dropdown menu and replaced it with “Trails” and placed it higher on the dropdown menu. In addition, our users were also confused when they saw the word “Join” on our dropdown menu as it was too vague. Our team re-defined it to “Join the Community” to match our website.

Visual Design.

As part of our iteration and re-design, we created a color style guide that would have earthy tones to match the mountains in the morning as the fog is still rolling out. Peppered with colorful tones you would find in foliage during the fall season.

Iterations.

The following step was iterations. Taking feedback from the users and implementing it into our design was crucial for the next part of our design process: the final design.

Redesign.

Our redesign after implementing user feedback for both website and mobile.

Takeaway.

Our ultimate lesson was to never assume.
While we as designers may automatically link certain terms and images,
it doesn’t mean users, or the general public will understand.
The goal of designers is to make user interactions as efficient and simple as possible.
This is what we aim to be with our designs and research.

Future Opportunities

With growth of AMC’s website and popularity comes with opportunities to improve
upon Patrick and Patricia’s experience as users. We hope to implement an interactive checklist/map for
individuals preparing to go hiking on a trail and for when they’re on the trail.
In addition to improving the experience, there are opportunities to increase revenue
with AMC merchandise and donations that come with AMC stickers as gifts.
Gifts they can place on their Hydroflasks, cars, and more which can come as a form of advertising.
